# Atlas Motivation

[Docs](../README.md) > [Design](./README.md) > Atlas Motivation

This document explains why Atlas exists and what gap it is meant to
close.

## The core problem

People across the United States are doing serious work to rebuild civic
life: organizing tenants, building worker power, expanding healthcare
access, advocating for transit, protecting water, strengthening
democracy, and building local institutions that hold communities
together.

The problem is not that this work does not exist. The problem is that it
can be genuinely hard to find, compare, and follow up on.

Important people and organizations are often visible only within their
immediate local context. Their work is scattered across local reporting,
organization websites, directories, interviews, research papers, and
public records. That makes local discovery slow, manual, and uneven.

If you want to find them, there is usually no single place to go. You
have to piece the picture together yourself.

## Why Atlas Was Created

Atlas was created to turn that fragmented public record into something
more usable: a source-linked map that people can actually work from.

The short version is simple: Atlas is that map.

The goal is to make it possible to go from:

- "Who is working on housing in this city?"
- "Who is building worker cooperatives in this region?"
- "Who should we talk to if we want credible local sources on this issue?"

to a structured set of leads grounded in public evidence.

Atlas exists because this research is too important to stay ad hoc.
Without a shared system, every journalist, organizer, researcher, and
producer has to start from scratch each time they enter a new place or a
new issue area.

## Why A Directory Alone Is Not Enough

A static directory would still leave the hardest part undone: finding
the right people and organizations in the first place.

That is why Atlas centers the autodiscovery pipeline. The pipeline is
not a side tool behind the product. It is the core mechanism that makes
the directory possible.

The pipeline takes a location and a set of issue areas, searches across
public sources, extracts structured information, merges duplicates, and
returns results that can be reviewed and revisited later.

Atlas was created to do three things together:

- Discover relevant people, organizations, and initiatives
- Structure that information in a consistent way
- Preserve source links so the resulting directory stays legible and credible

## What Atlas Makes Easier

Atlas is meant to lower the cost of local civic discovery.

It helps teams and individuals:

- Find people and organizations working on specific issues in a place
- Build research leads faster
- Understand who is doing what in a city or region
- Revisit the same place later without starting over
- Trace every entry back to the public record

In practice, that means turning days of scattered manual searching into
a more structured and repeatable process.

## Why This Matters To Rebuilding America

Atlas is maintained by The Rebuilding America Project because it supports
the broader mission of surfacing the people, organizations, and ideas
rebuilding public life across the country.

For Rebuilding America, this matters operationally and editorially:

- It helps identify credible local people to feature, interview, or learn from
- It helps compare work across issue areas and places
- It creates a reusable research asset instead of one-off investigation

At the same time, Atlas is meant to stand on its own as a public,
open-source resource. Its value is not limited to one organization or
one media effort.

It should be useful even to people who have never heard of Rebuilding
America.

## Design Principle

Atlas starts from a simple belief:

If the public record already contains evidence of who is rebuilding a
place, we should be able to discover, organize, and revisit that
information without redoing the same manual search every time.

That is the core motivation behind the project.
