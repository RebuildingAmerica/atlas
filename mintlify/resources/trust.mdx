## Overview

Atlas is designed around a simple principle: **every claim traces back to a public source**. This is not an afterthought — it's the core mechanism that makes Atlas trustworthy.

## Source attribution

Every entity in Atlas is backed by at least one source — the news article, nonprofit listing, organizational website, or public record where Atlas found the information. When you look at an entry, you see:

- **What** was published (title, publication)
- **When** it was published (date)
- **Where** to verify it (URL)
- **What was said** (extraction context — the specific passage that mentioned this entity)

This means you never have to take Atlas at its word. You can click through to the original source and verify.

## Freshness signals

Source dates tell you how current the information is:

- An entity backed by a March 2026 article is more actionable than one last mentioned in 2023
- `first_seen` tells you when Atlas first discovered an entity
- `last_seen` tells you when the most recent source was added

When source dates are old, it may mean the entity is no longer active — or it may mean Atlas hasn't run recent discovery in that area.

## Multi-source corroboration

Entities that appear in multiple independent sources earn higher confidence:

- 1 source: Atlas found a mention, but it's a single data point
- 3+ sources: Multiple independent publications confirm this entity's work
- The [ranking system](/how-it-works) scores entities higher when they have more independent sources

## Community corrections

Atlas provides anonymous flagging to help maintain data quality:

- **Entity flags** — report that an entry is stale, incorrect, or inappropriate
- **Source flags** — report that a source link is broken or misattributed

Flags don't require an account. They're reviewed by Atlas maintainers and used to update or remove entries. See [Sources & flags](/api-reference/guides/sources-flags) for the API.

## What Atlas doesn't do

- Atlas does not independently verify claims made in sources
- Atlas does not rank entities by "importance" or "impact" — ranking is based on source density, recency, and specificity
- Atlas does not assert opinions — it documents, links, and organizes what's already in the public record
